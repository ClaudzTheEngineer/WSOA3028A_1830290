Interacting with Interactivity

by Claudio Surmon

Interactivity as defined by the Oxford dictionary is "Any communication between two or more individuals that is dynamically shaped by the participants of the exchange", however in relation to computers and the web at large, interactivity refers more to the dialog that is exchanged between a user (human) and a computer. The range of interactivity a program can have is vast, from the high end with video games, down to the low end with business applications.

Web Design

Interactivity is a very important element in web-design (without it websites would not work). However, elevated amounts of it can make the user-experience better and more engaging, encouraging uses to not only stay longer but return later.

Gamification

Interactivity allows for the user to be more engaged with the task they have at hand. This can clearly seen in the addictive nature of video games and the dopamine kicks they give, however the principles that keep people glued to their games are being used in other fields aswell.
In recent years, companies have realised that the use of game techniques in their non-game applications and marketing can be a powerful tool. By implementing features that promote interactivity (such as point scoring, leveling systems etc) user engagement with the product increases. Companies such as the Botswana Insurance Company are utilising a mobile game focused on businesses to promote both their products and better spending habits.
These practices could be used in more predatory contexts to create damaging habits through the use of Skinner boxes and similar. A skinner box (or Operant conditioning chamber) is a device that conditions a user to do repeated actions on a schedule to recieve some form of reward. By conditioning a user, these users could be abused in a similar vein to how some mobile app developers prey on those with poor gambling habits or impatience with microtransactions.
