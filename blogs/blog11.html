Divide by zero

by Claudio Surmon

The internet is meant to be the great equaliser. Where everyone is equal, and the prejudice of the real world is gone. However, one of the major limiting factors is the devices through which we access the internet Because of the necessity for an internet connection and devices, not everyone has the same level of access globally. Even though the original intention was good, cracks have formed, this is known as the digital divide. But its more than just access, it extends to the understanding and the creation of content on the internet.

The digital divide appears in multiple ways; in how certain areas of the world cannot afford internet connections, where certain age groups cannot understand the how these systems work, or even just entire areas being restricted from their access. For instance, there are areas of Africa where for a yearly subscription to broadband internet costs more than the average yearly salary, up to 50 times as much! (citation to that towards thing).

There are numerous factors that could affect these performance metrics. Far too many to list in their entirety. As such I will be speculating why these problems perpetuate themselves.

1. It was created in English speaking country for those people

The primary language used on the internet is English, without question, W3Techs estimated the proportion of languages used in the top 10 million websites to be 59.6% English, with the second most being Russian with 8.6%. This probably came from the fact that the internet was created in an English-speaking country for an English-Speaking country. This causes the platform to far more friendly towards English speaking users and dissuading other language users.

This is also visible in the actual framework of the internet, where most coding languages used are English based, using the Latin alphabet and English words (like for, if, while etc). This makes it far harder for non-native English speakers to learn to code as they must use words or even characters, they are not familiar with.

Strangely enough, even though a lot of English based languages are used, some weren’t created in a English speaking territory, like Ruby being from Japan, Lua from Brazil and Python from the Netherlands. In these cases, the choice of a foreign language being used for the coding was due to wanting to appeal to an international audience. This helps perpetuate the use of English almost exclusively.

2. Other countries joined in later, they had less time to create content.

If you’re in south Africa accessing a website in the united states, its not like you must go outside get a compass and point to the USA to get signal, in general internet access is all or nothing. If you’re in range of a tower, the location of a website doesn’t matter. (There are cases where proximity to the server matters, like gaming, but this is more a time factor over a product quality factor) As such you as a consumer would want to use the best services on the internet, regardless of location. However, this causes the side-effect of the largest and best websites (usually US based) to grow larger and reinforces their monopoly.

For example, there are other online video-streaming services besides YouTube, but because YouTube has more users, more content creators, better monetisation among other reasons, people gravitate to this platform even with all the controversy surrounding it. For instance, one of the largest YouTuber’s, PewDiePie, probably chose YouTube as it was the largest platform at the time and still is. Even though the platform “Dailymotion” based in France was more localised to him.

3. Good things attract good people

Biases reinforce biases. When there is some form of hierarchy with regards to job opportunities, generally people will want to climb the ladder, as it currently stands Europe and the US have the best prospects when it comes to coding jobs, and as such would attract the best talent regardless of origin. Combine this with companies in theses countries poaching the best talent from around the world and you get a situation where the best gets better and the worst get worse. These people aren’t necessarily self-serving.

The general populous have foresight. In general, they want the best for people they love; thus, people fear capping their success. You don’t want pay-as-you-go success. People generally want uncapped success. The telemarketer in your head is always urging you to upgrade. And as such the best amongst us will move to where they can fully develop themselves.

So, is it even possible or worth it to bridge the gap created by the digital divide, will it always be a divide by zero? I guess we’ll find out.
